Most homeowners [are paying](/you-are-paying-more-for-home-insurance-but-covered-for-less/) for two products they don't fully understand, and the overlap between them is costing people real money every single year.

I'll be honest: after 14 years reviewing claims, I assumed I knew exactly where the line was between [homeowners insurance](/homeowners-insurance-binder/) and a home warranty. Then I spent the last few months digging into actual policy language, talking to homeowners who'd filed claims under both, and going through denial letters from warranty companies. What surprised me was how often people file under the wrong product, get denied, and then assume they have no coverage at all. They do. They just need to know where to look.

Here's the short version before we get into the weeds. Homeowners insurance exists to protect you from sudden, unexpected disasters: a tree through your roof, a fire, a burst pipe. A [home warranty](/home-insurance-vs-home-warranty-explained/) is a service contract covering mechanical breakdown of systems and appliances over time. These two products were never meant to duplicate each other. But they do overlap in frustrating ways, particularly around water damage, and that's where people lose.

## What Each Product Actually Does

Homeowners insurance is regulated by your state's insurance department (you can look up your state's regulator through the [NAIC's state map](https://www.naic.org/state_web_map.htm)) and pays out when a covered peril causes damage. The key word is "peril." Your standard HO-3 policy covers fire, wind, hail, lightning, theft, vandalism, and certain types of water damage. It does not cover flooding (that's a separate NFIP or private flood policy) and it does not cover equipment that just... wears out.

A home warranty is not insurance at all. It's a service contract. The company promises to send a technician to diagnose and repair or replace covered items when they fail due to normal wear and tear. That sounds great. In practice, there are service call fees ($75 to $125 per visit is typical), coverage caps per item, and an extensive list of conditions that can void a claim.

The [Insurance Information Institute](https://www.iii.org/) puts the average annual homeowners insurance premium at roughly $1,915 nationally as of 2026, though that varies wildly by state. A home warranty runs somewhere between $400 and $700 per year depending on coverage tier, with add-ons (pool equipment, second refrigerators, well pumps) piling on fast.

That pre-existing condition row deserves more attention than it gets. I've seen warranty claim denials where the company hired its own technician, that technician noted rust or sediment inside a water heater, and that was it: claim denied as pre-existing. The homeowner had owned the water heater for 18 months. The denial stuck because the contract language was air-tight.

## Where They Overlap (and Where You Can Fall Through the Gap)

Water damage is the battleground. Let's say your dishwasher's supply line fails overnight. You wake up to standing water, warped hardwood, and a damaged cabinet base.

Your homeowners insurance should cover the structural damage (floors, cabinets, drywall) because a sudden pipe failure typically qualifies as a covered peril. The appliance itself? Not the insurer's problem. That's where the home warranty comes in, potentially covering the dishwasher repair or replacement. In theory, they work together perfectly. In practice, each company may say the other is responsible for part of it.

Scenario one: A homeowner in Columbus, Ohio files under her home warranty for a failed HVAC compressor. The warranty technician diagnoses it correctly, but notes the filter hadn't been changed in over a year. The company denies the claim, citing improper maintenance. She calls me furious. Her homeowners insurer won't touch it because there's no peril involved. She's out roughly $2,400 for a replacement compressor. This scenario plays out constantly. She had both products and recovered nothing.

Scenario two: A reader in Austin, Texas had a pipe burst inside his wall last January during an unusual freeze. His homeowners insurer covered the water damage repair (about $8,700 in drywall and flooring) minus his $1,000 deductible. His home warranty covered the pipe repair itself after a $100 service fee. Total out-of-pocket: about $1,100. The system worked because the cause was clearly sudden and the pipe failure was documented independently by a plumber before any warranty technician arrived. Timing and documentation matter enormously.

Scenario three: Someone buys a house, skips the home warranty, and three months later the furnace fails. Homeowners insurance won't cover mechanical breakdown. Repair bill: $3,200. If they'd added a home warranty at closing (often $500 to $600 for the first year), the replacement would have been mostly covered minus the service fee. The math isn't always favorable for warranties, but for major systems in aging homes, it often is.

## The Claims Experience Is Not Equal

I want to be direct here because I spent years on the insurance side and I've watched people go through both processes. Homeowners insurance claims, for covered perils with solid documentation, tend to resolve more predictably. State insurance regulators enforce bad faith laws. Adjusters have training standards. You can appeal to your state's department of insurance if you're denied unfairly.

Home warranty claims exist in a murkier space. The warranty industry is not uniformly regulated across states. Some states treat these contracts as insurance; others treat them as ordinary service contracts with almost no consumer protection backstop. The company can send their preferred technician (not yours), and that technician's report becomes the basis for a claim decision you may have very little power to challenge.

What I do know from reviewing what feels like thousands of water-damage files: people who kept appliance maintenance records (the filter change receipts, the HVAC tune-up invoices) had dramatically better outcomes with warranty claims. The research on exact denial rates is mixed across providers, but I've seen internal estimates suggesting 25 to 35 percent of warranty claims get denied or partially denied on first submission. That's not a small number.

## How to Decide What You Actually Need

Most homeowners should carry both, but not without scrutiny.

If your home is under 10 years old with newer systems and appliances still under manufacturer warranty, the value of a home warranty is genuinely questionable. You're paying for coverage that may duplicate what you already have for free. I'd wait.

If your home is 15-plus years old with original HVAC, original water heater, and original appliances, a home warranty starts to make financial sense, but read the contract language on pre-existing conditions before you sign. Ask specifically: "If your technician finds evidence of wear during inspection, will that disqualify my claim?" Get the answer in writing.

For the homeowners insurance side, the [Insurance Information Institute](https://www.iii.org/) recommends reviewing your dwelling coverage limit annually to make sure it reflects current construction costs in your area, which have climbed significantly since 2021. Underinsurance is a real problem right now. Replacement cost coverage beats actual cash value every time in my experience, especially on roofs and older systems.

One practical move I always suggest: build a home inventory. A free app like Encircle or even just a video walkthrough on your phone stored in the cloud can cut your claims time in half. You can also keep physical documents in a [fireproof document safe](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=fireproof+document+safe) (affiliate link, site may earn a commission) so your warranty contracts and maintenance receipts survive the same disaster you're filing a claim about. That irony happens more than you'd think.
