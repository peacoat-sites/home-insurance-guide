Here's a question most people never think to ask their insurance agent: does my policy cover what it would actually cost to *rebuild* my home today?

That's the number that matters. Not your home's market value. Not what you paid for it. What it costs, per square foot, to reconstruct it with current materials and labor.

## The Dwelling Coverage Problem

Your dwelling coverage limit should equal your home's *replacement cost* -- what it costs to rebuild from scratch. Many homeowners have policies written years ago that never kept pace with construction cost inflation. In some markets, building costs have risen 40-60% since 2020.

If your limit is $300,000 and rebuilding costs $420,000, you're covering a $120,000 gap yourself.

## How to Calculate the Right Amount

A few ways to find your replacement cost:

- **Ask your insurer for a replacement cost estimator** -- most run one automatically at renewal
- **Use an online calculator** like those from Marshall & Swift or CoreLogic
- **Hire a local contractor** for a rough estimate per square foot, then multiply by your home's square footage

Most insurers also offer *extended replacement cost* (typically 20-50% buffer above your limit) or *guaranteed replacement cost* (covers the full rebuild no matter what). These add a modest premium but eliminate the coverage gap.

## What Else Are You Covering?

Beyond the structure itself, your standard HO-3 policy covers:

- **Other structures** (fence, detached garage, shed): usually 10% of dwelling limit
- **Personal property** (furniture, electronics, clothing): usually 50-70%
- **Liability** (if someone gets hurt on your property): typically $100,000-$300,000
- **Loss of use** (hotel and living costs if your home is uninhabitable): usually 20%

For most homeowners, $100,000 in liability is too low. Umbrella policies that add $1 million+ in coverage typically cost $150-$300/year.

## The Cheapest Coverage Isn't the Right Coverage

A policy that costs $200 less per year but leaves you $100,000 short after a fire isn't a bargain. The goal is matching your actual exposure -- not hitting a price point.

Review your dwelling limit every year at renewal, especially in an active construction market. Five minutes now can save you tens of thousands after a loss.
