Starting July 1, 2026, something quietly shifted in Colorado that the insurance industry didn't exactly advertise. For the first time in the country, homeowners in this state have a legal right to see the wildfire risk score an insurer used to price their policy, spike their premium, or drop them entirely. That score, generated by proprietary models most companies treated as a trade secret, is now something you can request, review, and formally dispute. Colorado's HB25-1182 is genuinely new ground, and if you own a home in this state, you need to understand what it actually does and what it still doesn't fix.

I'll be honest: when I spent 14 years reviewing claims, risk scoring was the part of this industry I found most opaque even from the inside. Underwriters would reference scores from vendors like Verisk or CoreLogic, and policyholders had no idea these numbers existed, let alone that they could determine whether a renewal letter arrived at all. Colorado just cracked that door open. Whether homeowners can actually walk through it depends on how the next few months play out.

## The Problem This Law Was Built to Solve

The numbers here are not abstractions. A homeowner near Idaho Springs watched their annual premium jump from $4,677 to $34,600 at a single renewal, a 740% increase reported by the Denver Post in April 2026. Statewide, Colorado homeowners insurance premiums doubled between 2020 and 2025. Mountain communities got hit the hardest, but the pressure has crept into foothills suburbs and even some urban areas that back up to open space.

What surprised me most in researching this was how much of that cost isn't actually wildfire. The Colorado Division of Insurance found that hail damage accounts for 26% to 54% of a typical annual homeowners premium in this state. Wildfire risk adds another 1% to 25% depending on county. So a home in, say, Clear Creek County might be carrying a heavy load from both. The wildfire score was the invisible piece. Hail is measurable and mapped, but wildfire risk models varied wildly by vendor, and consumers had no window into what variables were being weighted.

| Cost Driver | Share of Annual Premium (CO) |
|---|---|
| Hail damage | 26% – 54% |
| Wildfire risk surcharge | 1% – 25% (varies by county) |
| All other factors | Remainder |

That 1% to 25% range sounds manageable until you realize it's sitting on top of a base premium that's already doubled in five years.

## What the Law Actually Requires (Read This Carefully)

HB25-1182, which took effect July 1, 2026, requires any insurer using a wildfire risk model to disclose your score in writing at every application, renewal, or non-renewal. Not on request. Automatically. According to the Colorado Senate Democrats' June 2026 announcement of the law, this disclosure requirement is triggered every time the insurer uses that model in a coverage decision.

The appeal rights are real and time-bound. If you receive a score you believe is wrong, based on outdated satellite data, a misclassified roof type, or failure to recognize your defensible space work, you can file a formal appeal. The insurer must acknowledge it within 10 calendar days and issue a final written decision within 30 days. Those are legal requirements, not service guidelines. Missing those windows is a violation.

There's also a mitigation piece with real teeth. Insurers using these models must now either reflect parcel-level mitigation work (defensible space clearing, Class A roofing, ember-resistant vents) in their scoring models, or provide documented discounts to policyholders who demonstrate those actions. As Latent Insurance noted in their July 2026 analysis of the law, this is the provision that could have the most financial impact for homeowners who've already invested in hardening their property and never saw a premium reduction.

## What This Means Compared to Your Neighboring States

I want to be direct about something that doesn't get enough attention. If you're reading this from Arizona, Utah, New Mexico, Wyoming, or Montana, none of this applies to you. As of July 2026, Colorado stands alone. Those states have no equivalent consumer rights around wildfire risk scoring. An insurer can use any model they want, charge whatever the market tolerates, and never tell you why.

That context matters because it shows how unusual Colorado's position actually is. Live Insurance News described HB25-1182 in July 2026 as "the most specific and actionable wildfire risk score law in the country." That's a real distinction. But it also means the implementation is still being worked out in real time. Insurers have had months to prepare, but consumer advocates I've tracked are already flagging questions about how appeal decisions will be documented and whether the Division of Insurance has enough staff to handle complaints if insurers start slow-walking their 30-day response windows.

## How to Actually Use These New Rights

This is where I'll push back a little on the celebratory framing some outlets have used. Having the right to see your score is meaningful. Knowing what to do with it is something else entirely.

If you get a score that seems wrong, the first step is documentation. Pull your permit records for any roof replacement. Get dated photos of your defensible space. If you've installed ember-resistant vents, find the product receipts and any contractor statements. The appeal process requires you to make a factual case, not just express disagreement. Vague appeals will get vague denials.

Second, understand that the score is one input, not the whole picture. Premier Mountain Insurance's June 2026 breakdown of the law points out that insurers still have discretion in how they weight scores against other underwriting factors. A successful appeal that lowers your score doesn't automatically guarantee a lower premium, though it should, and you'd have grounds to push back if it doesn't translate to any pricing relief.

Third, talk to an independent agent who works in Colorado's high-risk markets specifically. The mitigation discount provisions are new enough that not every insurer has fully implemented them yet. An agent who writes a lot of mountain and foothills business will know which carriers are actually honoring the spirit of these requirements and which are technically complying while dragging their feet.

## The Limits Nobody's Talking About

Here's the part that gets left out of the positive press. HB25-1182 does not cap what insurers can charge. It does not prevent non-renewals. It does not require an insurer to stay in a market they've decided to exit. And the research is genuinely mixed on whether transparency laws alone move premiums. California has had significant wildfire insurance disclosure debates for years, and their market is arguably in worse shape than Colorado's right now.

What the law does is create a factual record and a process. That's not nothing. For a homeowner facing a 740% spike, having a documented score, a formal appeal, and a 30-day response requirement gives you something you didn't have on June 30, 2026. Whether that translates to actual savings depends on whether the score was actually wrong and whether your insurer engages honestly with the process.

I'd encourage every Colorado homeowner with a property in a wildfire-adjacent area to request their score now, even if their premium hasn't spiked. Build a baseline. Document your mitigation work. If you get a number that doesn't reflect what you've invested in your property, you now have a legal mechanism to challenge it. Use it early and use it with documentation. And because insurance law and individual policies vary significantly, consulting a licensed insurance professional or an attorney familiar with Colorado property insurance is worth the time before you file a formal appeal.
