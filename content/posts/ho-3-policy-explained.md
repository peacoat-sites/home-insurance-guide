Most homeowners have an HO-3 policy and couldn't tell you what that means. Not because they're careless, because no one ever explained it in plain language. Let me fix that.

The HO-3 is the standard homeowner policy sold across the United States. If you bought your home with a mortgage, there's a very good chance your lender required you to get one. It's the industry default, which sounds like a compliment. Sometimes it is. Sometimes it means you have exactly as much coverage as your insurer was required to offer, not a dollar more.

Here's what I tell people when they're sitting down with a new policy for the first time: the number on the front page isn't the important part. What matters is *how* the policy covers your home versus your belongings, because the HO-3 treats them very differently. That difference has real consequences when something goes wrong.

## Open Perils vs. Named Perils: The Core Distinction

Your home itself (the structure, the attached garage, the built-in cabinets) is covered on what's called an "open perils" or "all-risk" basis. That means the policy covers *any* cause of damage unless it's specifically excluded. Fire, wind, falling tree limbs, a car driving into your living room wall, covered, because nothing in the policy explicitly rules them out.

Your personal property (furniture, clothing, electronics, appliances) is covered on a "named perils" basis. The policy lists exactly what it will cover: fire, theft, vandalism, windstorm, and about a dozen other events. If something damages your stuff and that cause isn't on the list, you don't have a claim. That's a meaningful gap, and a lot of policyholders only discover it after the fact.

I saw this play out repeatedly. A homeowner's chest freezer fails while they're on vacation. They come home to $800 in ruined food and a soggy basement floor. The floor gets covered under the structural portion of the policy. The food? Almost certainly not, because power outage isn't a named peril under standard personal property coverage. The freezer itself? Also likely not. Mechanical breakdown isn't on the list.

This isn't a flaw in the HO-3, exactly. It's a design choice. But you should know it going in.

The policy breaks down into several coverage components, each with its own limit and its own rules.

**Dwelling coverage (Coverage A)** pays to repair or rebuild your home's structure. The number you want here isn't your purchase price or your home's market value. It's the *replacement cost*: what it would actually cost to rebuild from scratch at current labor and material prices. In 2026, with construction costs still elevated in most markets, a lot of homeowners are significantly underinsured on this number without realizing it.

**Other structures (Coverage B)** covers detached garages, fences, sheds. It's typically set at 10% of your dwelling coverage automatically. For most people that's fine. If you have a detached workshop you've invested real money in, it's worth checking whether that 10% actually covers it.

**Personal property (Coverage C)** is where things get complicated. Most policies set this at 50% to 70% of your dwelling coverage, which sounds like a lot until you actually inventory everything you own. Jewelry, electronics, and collectibles often have sub-limits (sometimes as low as $1,500 for jewelry theft) that are buried in the policy language. If you own anything valuable in those categories, you need a separate scheduled endorsement.

**Loss of use (Coverage D)** pays your additional living expenses if your home is uninhabitable after a covered loss. Hotel bills, restaurant meals above what you'd normally spend, short-term rental costs. This one saves people financially after major losses and gets surprisingly little attention at purchase time.

**Liability (Coverage E)** and **medical payments (Coverage F)** cover you if someone is injured on your property or you accidentally cause damage to someone else's property. Standard liability limits are often $100,000, which I'd argue is too low for most homeowners today. Umbrella policies exist for a reason.

Two major exclusions you absolutely need to understand: **flood** and **earthquake**. Neither is covered under any standard HO-3. Not a little covered, not partially covered. Zero. These require separate policies entirely, and the confusion around this costs homeowners real money every single year.

## Replacement Cost vs. Actual Cash Value

This is where I've seen more claim disappointment than anywhere else. It comes down to two words: depreciation.

Replacement cost coverage pays what it costs to replace the damaged item with something comparable today. Actual cash value (ACV) pays replacement cost minus depreciation. If your 8-year-old roof is destroyed in a hailstorm, ACV coverage might pay you 40% of what a new roof costs, because the adjuster calculated that a 20-year roof with 8 years of use has 60% depreciation. The rest comes out of your pocket.

Most HO-3 policies cover the dwelling structure at replacement cost. Personal property is trickier. Some policies default to ACV for contents unless you explicitly upgrade. Check your declarations page for the phrase "replacement cost contents" or "RC contents." If you don't see it, ask.

The [IBHS home fortification guides](https://ibhs.org/) are mainly focused on mitigation, but they include genuinely useful context on how construction quality affects replacement cost calculations. Worth a read if you're unsure whether your Coverage A limit is realistic.

## How to Actually Read Your Policy

Pull out your declarations page. That's the summary document, usually one or two pages, that lists your coverage limits, your deductibles, and your premium. This is not the same as the full policy, which might run 40 pages.

Your declarations page will show you:

- Coverage A limit (your dwelling coverage)
- Your deductible (and whether you have a separate, higher deductible for wind or hail)
- Whether personal property is replacement cost or ACV
- Any endorsements you've added

Wind and hail deductibles are increasingly common in coastal states and tornado-prone areas. They're often percentage-based rather than a flat dollar amount, so a "2% deductible" on a $400,000 home means you're absorbing the first $8,000 of any wind or hail claim yourself. That surprises people.

The [NAIC](https://www.naic.org/) publishes a free homeowner insurance shopping guide that explains how to compare policy terms across insurers. It's dry reading but accurate, and it includes a glossary that actually helps with the jargon.

Before you renew each year, take 15 minutes to verify your dwelling coverage limit still reflects current rebuild costs. Do a rough home inventory (there are decent apps for this, like [Encircle](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=home+inventory+app) or simply photos stored in cloud backup) and make sure your personal property limit still makes sense. Store those records somewhere outside your home, ideally in a fireproof document safe like the [SentrySafe SFW123GDC](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=SentrySafe+fireproof+document+safe) or a secure cloud folder.
