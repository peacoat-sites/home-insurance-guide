Most homeowners have no idea their hail claim has already been partially decided before they even call their insurer. The adjuster arriving at your house isn't walking in neutral. They're working from a damage threshold matrix, a regional hail history database, and years of training on how to separate "cosmetic" damage from "functional" damage. That distinction alone determines whether you get a check or a denial letter.

I spent 14 years on the other side of that equation. Here's what I actually saw.

---

## What Your Policy Says vs. What It Pays
As of June 2026, 
Hail damage is covered under the dwelling portion of a standard HO-3 policy. That's true and mostly consistent across carriers. But there's a line buried in most policies, sometimes just a sentence or two, about "cosmetic damage exclusions." Insurers started adding these around 2012-2014 in response to high claim volume in hail-prone states like Texas, Colorado, and Oklahoma. What surprised me, even after years of reviewing claims, was how broadly some carriers define "cosmetic."

A dented metal roof that's lost its protective coating? Cosmetic, per some adjusters. A fiberglass shingle that's bruised and cracked below the surface granules? Could go either way. Gutters with impact marks but no functional loss? Almost always cosmetic. The thing is, cosmetic damage left untreated becomes functional damage in three to five years when moisture gets in. The policy won't care about that future claim's origin story.

Pull out your actual declarations page right now if you're in a hail-prone area and search for "cosmetic damage exclusion" or "market value exclusion." If it's in there, you're playing a different game than you might think.

One more thing about policy language: your deductible. A lot of policies issued after 2008 have a separate wind/hail deductible expressed as a percentage of your dwelling coverage, not a flat dollar amount. One percent of a $400,000 home is $4,000. Two percent is $8,000. I've seen homeowners discover this mid-claim when they expected to pay $1,000 and found out the real number was triple that.

Don't wait. This is the one area where I'll give you an unambiguous answer: document everything before anything is touched, tarped, or repaired.

Walk the exterior of your house within 24 hours of the storm if it's safe. You're looking for spatter marks on painted wood, dents in aluminum fascia, impact points on downspouts, dimpling on AC condenser fins, and most importantly, bruising on your shingles. Bruising shows up as soft spots where granules have been knocked loose, leaving a darker circular indentation. Run your hand across the shingles if you can safely access a lower slope. A bruised shingle feels soft in a way an intact one doesn't.

Take video, not just photos. Walk the whole perimeter, narrate what you're seeing, timestamp it. Upload it to cloud storage immediately. This matters because hail damage documentation is time-sensitive, even if filing isn't.

Then check your gutters and downspouts for granule accumulation. A heavy granule deposit after a storm that matches your shingle color is corroborating evidence.

I'd also strongly suggest picking up a [water leak sensor](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=water+leak+sensor&tag=contentportfo-20) to place in your attic or near vulnerable ceiling areas if you suspect roof penetration. A timestamped alert showing active moisture intrusion strengthens a claim considerably. *(The site may earn a commission on this link.)*

File your claim promptly. Don't sign anything quickly, though. Call the number on your declarations page, get a claim number, and ask for an estimated timeline for an adjuster visit.

## The Adjuster Visit: What's Actually Happening

The adjuster assigned to your claim may be a staff adjuster (employed by your insurer) or an independent adjuster contracted for surge capacity after a major storm event. Independent adjusters aren't necessarily worse, but their incentives are different and their familiarity with your specific policy forms may be thinner.

What they're doing on your roof is looking for functional damage: evidence that the hail impact has compromised the waterproofing ability of the material. For asphalt shingles, that means fractured mats, not just surface granule loss. For metal roofing, it means dents that break the coating. The Insurance Information Institute has solid plain-language breakdowns of [how claim assessments work](https://www.iii.org/) if you want to understand the framework adjusters are actually using.

Here's what I'll be honest about: the first adjuster assessment isn't always wrong, but it isn't always right either. I reviewed plenty of initial assessments during my time that missed secondary damage, particularly on older roofs where hail impact interacts with pre-existing wear in complicated ways. If your claim comes back as cosmetic-only or partially denied and the damage is significant, get a second opinion from a licensed public adjuster or a roofing contractor who does insurance work (not the same as a storm chaser, more on that below).

Ask the adjuster directly: "Is any of this being classified as cosmetic rather than functional?" Make them say it out loud. Document their answer.

## The Storm Chaser Problem

After major hail events, your neighborhood will fill up with roofing contractors within 48 hours, some from states away. A lot of them are legitimate. Some are running what the industry calls "assignment of benefits" schemes, where they get you to sign over your insurance rights so they can deal directly with your insurer, and then overbill aggressively, creating legal and coverage headaches that follow you for years.

Red flags: anyone who asks you to sign before an adjuster has visited, anyone who "guarantees" they'll get your full roof replaced regardless of inspection, anyone who offers to waive your deductible (that's insurance fraud in most states, and your carrier will pursue it).

The IBHS has published home fortification guides and post-storm inspection resources at [ibhs.org](https://ibhs.org/) that are worth bookmarking before a storm season, not after.

## If Your Claim Gets Denied or Underpaid

A denial isn't final. You have several paths.

First, request the insurer's written explanation of the denial with specific policy language cited. They're required to provide this. Read it carefully. "Cosmetic damage exclusion" and "pre-existing condition" are the two most common denial grounds for hail claims, and both are sometimes applied incorrectly or overly broadly.

Second, hire a licensed public adjuster if the claim value justifies it. They typically charge 10-15% of the final settlement, so on a $15,000 roof claim, that's real money, but on a $50,000+ claim involving roof, siding, and structural elements, it often more than pays for itself. Check your state's department of insurance website to verify licensing.

Third, invoke your policy's appraisal clause. Most HO-3 policies include this provision for disputes about the amount of loss. Both sides hire an appraiser, those two appoint an umpire, and the umpire's decision binds both parties. It's not litigation, but it's not nothing either.

And yes, you can file a complaint with your state's department of insurance. Carriers notice this. It creates a paper trail that can influence how disputed claims get handled internally.
