Most Texas homeowners I've talked to assume their policy works like their neighbor's policy in Ohio or Colorado. It doesn't. Texas is one of the few states where insurers can use policy forms that aren't used anywhere else in the country, where the wind and hail deductible system is genuinely unlike anything you'll encounter in other markets, and where a growing number of carriers are quietly refusing to renew policies in entire ZIP codes without much explanation. If you're buying a home here, renewing a policy, or just wondering why your premium jumped 30% this year, you need to understand how this market actually works. Not the brochure version.

## Why Texas Home Insurance Is Its Own Animal

I'll be honest: when I started reviewing Texas claims early in my career, I thought the state's insurance market was just "more expensive" because of weather. That's true but wildly incomplete. Texas operates under a system called a "file and use" regulatory environment, which means insurers can introduce new rates and forms and immediately start selling them before the Texas Department of Insurance formally approves them. Compare that to states where every rate change requires prior approval, and you start to see why premiums here can shift faster and more dramatically than almost anywhere else.

What surprised me was learning how many Texans don't realize their policy might be written on a non-standard form. Texas has its own homeowners policy forms, including the HO-A, HO-B, and HO-C, and the differences between them are significant. The HO-A is a named perils policy, meaning it only covers the specific causes of loss listed in the document. The HO-B covers open perils on the dwelling and named perils on personal property. The HO-C, closest to what most people outside Texas think of as a "standard" policy, covers open perils on both. Many buyers don't know which one they have until they file a claim and find out their loss wasn't listed.

The Texas market is also shaped by the Texas Windstorm Insurance Association (TWIA), a state-created insurer of last resort that provides wind and hail coverage to homeowners in coastal counties who can't get it from private carriers. If you live in Galveston, Corpus Christi, or anywhere along the Gulf Coast, there's a real chance your wind coverage comes from TWIA while your fire and theft coverage comes from a private insurer. Two policies, two deductibles, two claims processes if a hurricane hits.

This is where I've seen Texas homeowners get genuinely blindsided. Elsewhere in the country, you might have a single flat deductible, say $1,000 or $2,500, that applies to almost any covered claim. In Texas, and especially in areas prone to severe weather, your policy almost certainly has a separate wind and hail deductible that works differently.

Instead of a flat dollar amount, this deductible is typically expressed as a percentage of your home's insured value. One percent, two percent, sometimes five percent. On a home insured for $400,000, a 2% wind and hail deductible means $8,000 comes out of your pocket before the insurer pays a dime on storm damage. If you've never had to file a hail claim, you might not even know that number is sitting in your policy.

The percentage can vary by county, by how close you are to the coast, and by the specific carrier. Some policies apply this deductible only when a named storm (a hurricane with an official name) causes the damage. Others apply it to any wind or hail event regardless of severity. These aren't minor distinctions. I've reviewed claims where homeowners expected to pay $1,000 and ended up owing $12,000 because of a percentage deductible they didn't fully understand.

Read your declarations page carefully. Find the line that says "wind" or "hurricane" deductible and look at whether it's a dollar amount or a percentage. If it's a percentage, do the math right now.

## Flood Is Not Covered. Full Stop.

I know this gets said a lot, but Texas is a state where people genuinely need to hear it again. Standard homeowner policies, every version of the HO-A, HO-B, and HO-C, exclude flood damage. Not "most flood damage." All of it.

Texas has some of the most flood-prone geography in the country. Houston in particular sits on clay soil that doesn't absorb water well, has limited natural drainage, and is crossed by multiple bayous that overflow regularly. Hurricane Harvey in 2017 dropped more than 60 inches of rain in some areas, and tens of thousands of homeowners discovered too late that they had no flood coverage. Many of those homes weren't in FEMA-designated high-risk flood zones. About 80% of Harvey flood victims reportedly didn't have flood insurance.

The National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) is the primary source of flood coverage for most homeowners, and private flood insurance options have expanded meaningfully in recent years. If you're in or near a flood-prone area, the question isn't whether flood insurance is worth it. It's which program offers better terms for your specific property. The Insurance Information Institute at iii.org has a solid breakdown of how NFIP and private flood policies compare, which is worth reading before you make that call.

## What Your Policy Probably Doesn't Cover (That You Think It Does)

Beyond flood, there are several Texas-specific coverage gaps worth knowing.

**Foundation movement.** Texas homes, particularly in Central Texas and the DFW Metroplex, sit on highly expansive clay soils that shrink and swell dramatically with moisture changes. Foundation movement is extraordinarily common. Standard homeowner policies almost universally exclude "settling, shrinking, bulging, or expansion" of foundations. This isn't a loophole, it's explicit policy language. Foundation repairs in Texas can cost anywhere from several thousand to tens of thousands of dollars depending on severity, and almost none of it is covered.

**Sewer backup and water line damage.** A burst pipe inside the home is usually covered. Water backing up through a drain or sewer line is not, unless you've specifically added a sewer backup endorsement. Damage from a broken water service line running from the street to your house may or may not be covered depending on where the break occurs and your specific policy language. These endorsements are generally inexpensive and often worth adding.

**Mold.** Texas has a complicated history with mold claims. After a wave of massive mold-related lawsuits in the early 2000s, many insurers significantly tightened mold coverage. Today, most policies cover mold only when it results directly from a covered water loss that was promptly addressed. Pre-existing mold, slow leaks, and humidity-related mold are typically excluded. Given Texas's humidity levels, this is a real exposure.

**Short-term rental use.** If you rent your home on Airbnb or VRBO, even occasionally, your standard homeowner policy may not cover losses that occur during rental periods. This is a growing issue. Talk to your agent before listing.

## How to Actually Compare Texas Home Insurance Policies

Step-by-step, here's how I'd approach buying or renewing coverage in Texas right now:

**Step 1: Know your policy form.** Before comparing prices, find out whether you're being quoted an HO-A, HO-B, or HO-C equivalent. A lower premium on a named-perils HO-A is not the same product as a higher premium on an open-perils policy.

**Step 2: Get the wind/hail deductible in writing, as a dollar amount.** Ask the agent to calculate what the percentage deductible represents in actual dollars on your coverage amount. Compare that number across quotes, not just the base premium.

**Step 3: Check the replacement cost methodology.** Does the policy use Replacement Cost Value (RCV) or Actual Cash Value (ACV) for your dwelling? For personal property? ACV applies depreciation, meaning older furniture or a 10-year-old roof gets paid out at a fraction of what replacement actually costs.

**Step 4: Ask specifically about the roof.** Many Texas insurers now apply ACV to roofs over a certain age, sometimes 10 or 15 years, even if the dwelling itself is covered at replacement cost. A hail claim on a 12-year-old roof under ACV settlement could leave you significantly short of full replacement cost.

**Step 5: Check the insurer's complaint ratio.** The Texas Department of Insurance publishes complaint data. A carrier with a persistently high complaint ratio relative to its market share is a yellow flag, especially for claims handling.

**Step 6: Document your home before you need to.** A home inventory app (many are free) or even a detailed video walkthrough stored in the cloud can dramatically speed up a personal property claim. ## Practical Things That Can Actually Lower Your Premium

The IBHS home fortification research at ibhs.org has documented specific construction features that reduce storm damage, and many Texas insurers recognize these with discounts. Fortified roof designation, impact-resistant roofing, hurricane straps on rafters, and reinforced garage doors are all worth investigating if you're building or doing major repairs.

Beyond construction, installing water leak sensors (like [these monitored options](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=water+leak+sensor+home)) can qualify you for discounts with some carriers and, more practically, can catch a slow leak before it becomes a mold or structural claim. A monitored alarm system, deadbolts, and updated electrical panels also typically generate credits.

Raising your deductible on the non-wind portion of your policy is another lever, though given the size of wind/hail deductibles already, I'd think carefully before also taking on a high flat deductible.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is homeowners insurance required by law in Texas?

No, Texas doesn't legally require you to carry homeowners insurance. However, if you have a mortgage, your lender almost certainly requires it as a loan condition. If you let coverage lapse, the lender can "force-place" insurance on your property, which is typically more expensive and covers only the structure (protecting the lender's collateral), not your personal belongings or liability.

### Why did my Texas home insurance premium jump so much this year?

The Texas market is under serious strain. Reinsurance costs (what insurers pay to insure themselves) have risen sharply after multiple billion-dollar weather events. Inflation drove up construction and materials costs, meaning replacement cost values increased. Some carriers have also repriced entire regions based on updated catastrophe modeling. The research here is mixed on whether Texas is in a full-blown "insurance crisis" like Florida, but the pressure on premiums is real and not likely to reverse quickly.

### What's the difference between TWIA and a standard homeowner policy?

TWIA is a residual market insurer created by the state to cover windstorm and hail damage in 14 designated coastal counties. It doesn't cover fire, theft, liability, or water damage. If your private carrier excludes wind in coastal areas, you'd buy a TWIA policy specifically for wind/hail and a separate "inland" policy for everything else. TWIA coverage limits and rates are set by the state.

### Does Texas homeowners insurance cover damage from a neighbor's tree falling on my house?

Generally, yes, if your policy covers the type of damage caused. If a neighbor's tree falls on your roof during a storm, your own homeowner policy typically covers the repair to your structure (minus your deductible), regardless of whether the neighbor was negligent. Whether you can recover that deductible from the neighbor depends on whether they were negligent (e.g., a known dead tree they ignored). This is a common source of neighbor disputes. Document everything.

### How much liability coverage do I actually need in Texas?

Standard policies typically include $100,000 in personal liability coverage. I'll be honest, that number hasn't kept pace with the cost of serious injuries or lawsuits. Many homeowners consider $300,000 to $500,000 as a more realistic floor, and if you have significant assets, a personal umbrella policy (usually sold separately) adds an additional layer of protection at a relatively low cost. Talk to a licensed agent about your specific situation before making that call.

The policy form, the deductible structure, the flood gap, the roof settlement method: these are the details that determine whether your coverage actually works when you need it most. Take an hour before your next renewal to sit down with your declarations page and actually read what you're paying for. If something is unclear, ask your agent to explain it in writing.
