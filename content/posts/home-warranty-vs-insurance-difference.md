Nearly half of all homeowners confuse home warranties and home insurance, according to a 2025 survey by the National Association of Home Builders ,  and [insurance companies](/best-homeowners-insurance-companies/) are quietly fine with that.

I spent 14 years on the claims side of a national insurer, reviewing thousands of denials, and I can tell you from personal experience: that confusion costs homeowners real money. I've seen people file claims for a broken furnace under their homeowner's policy, get denied, and then realize they let their [home warranty](/homeowners-insurance-vs-home-warranty/) lapse six months earlier because they thought the insurance "covered everything." That's a rough day. I've also seen the opposite: someone paying $65 a month for a home warranty on a brand-new construction home where the builder warranty already covered the same equipment for another four years. Double-paying for nothing.

The distinction is genuinely simple once someone explains it honestly. Most explanations don't, because the industry benefits from the blur.

## What Each One Actually Covers

[Home insurance](/home-insurance-trampoline-exclusion/) is a financial protection product against catastrophic loss. Your house burns down, a tree falls through the roof, someone breaks in and steals your electronics ,  that's what insurance is built for. It protects the structure, your personal belongings, and (critically) your liability if someone gets hurt on your property. What it does not do, and was never designed to do, is pay for things that wear out over time. A 20-year-old water heater that finally gives up is not a covered loss. It's maintenance. Your adjuster will tell you that, and they'll be right.

Home warranties are service contracts, not insurance products. That's not just a semantic difference. It matters legally and practically. A warranty company promises to repair or replace specific systems and appliances when they break down due to normal wear. Your HVAC dies in July. Your dishwasher stops draining. Your garbage disposal sounds like a cement mixer. Those are warranty situations. The warranty company sends a technician (one they've contracted with, not one you pick), diagnoses the problem, and either fixes it or replaces the unit up to a stated dollar cap. You pay a service call fee, typically $75–$125, and the warranty absorbs the rest, up to whatever the contract limit is.

What most people don't realize is that the "covered" list in a warranty is much shorter than the marketing makes it look. Pre-existing conditions are almost universally excluded. Improper installation? Excluded. Lack of maintenance? Excluded. And that last one is doing a lot of heavy lifting in denial letters.

## Where the Gaps Hide (And Where I've Personally Watched Claims Fall Apart)

Here's where I'd ask you to slow down, because this is the part that actually saves money.

The single most common misunderstanding I dealt with as an adjuster: water damage. Homeowners assume any water damage is covered by insurance. It is not. Water that comes from a sudden, accidental event (pipe bursts, appliance malfunctions unexpectedly) is typically covered. Water damage from a slow leak you didn't notice for six months? Almost certainly denied as a "maintenance issue." The same leak, if it's in your pipes rather than caused by a storm, may or may not be covered under your home warranty depending on whether plumbing is in your plan.

I once reviewed a claim where a homeowner had a slow slab leak that went undetected for about eight months. The damage to the foundation and flooring was around $34,000. The insurance company denied it because the leak was gradual and the homeowner "should have known." The home warranty denied it because foundation damage isn't a covered system. That homeowner was stuck.

The [IBHS home fortification guides](https://ibhs.org/) are actually useful here ,  not just for storm prep, but because following their maintenance recommendations creates a documented record that can support your position if an insurer tries to claim negligence. Keeping maintenance records isn't paranoia. It's leverage.

A few specific gap scenarios that trip people up:

**Scenario 1:** Roof is 15 years old. Wind takes off a section of shingles in a storm.
Insurance claim → Adjuster finds pre-existing wear and tear → Insurer pays for storm damage only, deducts for depreciation on worn shingles → Homeowner gets $1,800 instead of the $6,400 repair estimate. The warranty wouldn't touch this at all, since roofs are often excluded or capped at minimal amounts.

**Scenario 2:** HVAC unit stops working in August. No storm, no external damage, just a failed compressor at 11 years old.
Insurance claim → Denied immediately (normal wear) → Warranty claim → Technician sent within 48 hours, compressor replaced, homeowner pays $100 service fee. Total cost: $100 instead of $3,200–$5,000.

**Scenario 3:** Pipe freezes and bursts in January, floods basement.
Insurance claim → Covered as sudden accidental damage → Insurance pays for water extraction, drywall, flooring → Warranty separately handles pipe repair → Homeowner pays deductible plus service fee, approximately $1,600 total out of pocket on a $28,000 damage event.

## Do You Actually Need Both?

Probably, if your home is more than 10 years old. I know that's not a satisfying answer but it's the honest one.

For newer homes (under 5 years old), builder warranties often cover major systems, and new appliances come with manufacturer warranties. Buying a separate home warranty on top of that is usually redundant, and I'd push back against any sales pitch that says otherwise.

For older homes, the calculus shifts. Aging HVAC, aging water heater, aging appliances ,  those are real risks that insurance won't touch. A home warranty, even an imperfect one, gives you a known service call cost instead of a surprise $4,000 bill. The [NAIC](https://www.naic.org/) notes that home warranties are not regulated the same way insurance products are in most states, which is exactly why you need to read the contract before you sign. The marketing brochure is not the contract.

One thing worth knowing: water leak sensors can help you catch slow leaks before they become uncoverable damage. They're inexpensive (models like [the Govee Water Sensor on Amazon](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=water+leak+sensor) run around $10–$20 each, and the site may earn a commission on purchases), and putting one near your water heater, under sinks, and near your washing machine connection is genuinely good risk management. Not flashy, but the kind of thing that means the difference between a covered sudden-loss claim and a denied gradual-damage claim.
