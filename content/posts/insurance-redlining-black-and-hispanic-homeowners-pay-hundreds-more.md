A Detroit homeowner and a neighbor three miles away, same house value, same [credit score](/home-insurance-credit-score-impact/), same 20-year-old home. One pays $1,768 more every single year for identical coverage. That's not a fluke in pricing or a quirk of one bad insurer. According to a major new report, it's a systemic pattern affecting millions of Black and Hispanic homeowners across the country, and it has a name most people hoped we'd left behind decades ago: redlining.

On July 15, 2026, the Consumer Federation of America released a report called "Redlined" that is already rattling state insurance commissioners and reigniting a debate about whether the [home insurance](/escrow-and-home-insurance/) industry has quietly rebuilt the discriminatory geography of the mid-20th century, just without the explicit maps. The CFA analyzed 216,151 test quotes across 32,978 residential ZIP codes in every U.S. state and Washington, D.C. They held everything constant: a $350,000 replacement-cost home, the same credit score, a 20-year-old structure. The only variable that predicted dramatically different premiums was whether you lived in a majority-Black or majority-Hispanic neighborhood. The results are difficult to explain away.

## The Numbers Are Not Subtle

I've spent 14 years reading claim files and rate schedules, and I'll be honest: I knew anecdotally that ZIP code played an outsized role in pricing. But the scale here is something else. Homeowners in majority-Black communities pay an average of 16% more in premiums, about $500 more each year, than comparable homeowners in majority-white communities. For Hispanic homeowners, the gap nearly doubles: 30% more on average, roughly $950 extra per year.

Those annual figures compound painfully over time. Across a 30-year mortgage, the CFA calculates that Black homeowners pay at least $15,000 more in cumulative premiums. Hispanic homeowners pay at least $28,500 more. That's real wealth, gone, for the exact same coverage protecting the exact same type of home.

The state-level breakdowns are where the numbers become almost hard to absorb. Here's how the worst-case gaps look by state and demographic group:

## How Insurers Justify This, and Why That Justification Falls Apart

What most people don't realize is that insurers rarely say they're pricing by race. They say they're pricing by risk, and that risk is expressed through ZIP codes, claims history in an area, distance from fire stations, local construction costs, and sometimes credit scores. None of those inputs has "race" written on it. But when you control for home value, credit score, and home age, the way the CFA study did, and the racial composition of a neighborhood still predicts premium levels, you have a serious problem.

The engineers and architects community has flagged this too, noting that the technical inputs insurers use for property risk assessment can embed historical inequities in infrastructure, maintenance patterns, and investment levels that trace directly back to decades of discriminatory lending and disinvestment. The ZIP code isn't neutral. It carries history.

Insurers will argue that this reflects real actuarial risk, that claims are genuinely higher in some of these areas. That [may have](/your-insurer-may-have-already-inspected-your-home-by-drone/) partial truth to it in specific markets. But the CFA's methodology was designed to test exactly this: if you hold the individual home's characteristics constant and the price still moves based on neighborhood demographics, the individual homeowner isn't being charged for their own risk. They're being charged for their neighbors' demographics. That's the definition of group-based penalization.

## What Regulators Can Actually Do, and the Gap Between "Can" and "Will"

State insurance commissioners have more power here than most people realize. Rate filings are submitted for regulatory approval in most states. Regulators can, in theory, reject pricing models that produce discriminatory outcomes. The pressure following this report is pushing several commissioners to say they'll take a closer look.

The problem is that "a closer look" has historically been slow and often toothless. The insurance industry spends considerable resources on state-level lobbying, and most state insurance departments are under-resourced relative to the complexity of actuarial review. As Real Estate News reported on July 15, the report is specifically calling for regulators to require insurers to test their own pricing models for disparate racial impact, and to publish the results. That's a meaningful ask because it shifts the burden of proof. Right now, an affected homeowner has almost no practical way to prove they're being overcharged compared to someone in a different ZIP code. Systemic analysis like the CFA's is the only tool that surfaces it.

No federal standard currently prohibits this pricing practice outright. The Fair Housing Act has been applied to insurance in some cases, but litigation is slow and outcomes are uncertain. This is a gap in consumer protection that has existed for a long time.

## What You Can Do Right Now

I want to be careful here: I can't tell you what any individual insurer will charge you, and your situation may differ meaningfully from the CFA's controlled scenarios. Consulting an independent insurance agent or a consumer advocacy organization in your state is genuinely advisable before making coverage decisions.

That said, here's what I'd tell a friend. Get comparison quotes from at least three insurers. The premium gap in the CFA data wasn't uniform across all companies, which means some insurers price more equitably than others. Ask your state insurance commissioner's office whether your insurer's rate filings have been reviewed for disparate impact. Some states are now publishing this information.

If you're in Michigan or Florida specifically, the gaps the CFA documented are severe enough that shopping your coverage aggressively, even mid-policy-term if your renewal is coming up, could save you thousands. And if your premium jumped significantly at your last renewal without a change in your claims history or home, ask your insurer in writing what specific factors drove the increase. You're entitled to that explanation in most states.

Document everything. I've seen homeowners win rate appeals simply by having their paperwork organized. Regulators and insurers both respond differently when someone is clearly prepared to escalate.

The "Redlined" report gives advocates, regulators, and affected homeowners a concrete, methodologically rigorous foundation to push back. That's new. For years this problem lived mostly in anecdote and suspicion. Now it's in 216,151 data points. Whether that translates into policy change depends entirely on whether the pressure holds, and whether the people most affected know what's being done to them.
