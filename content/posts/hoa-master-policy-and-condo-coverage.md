I'll be honest: the relationship between an HOA master policy and your individual condo insurance is one of the most genuinely confusing areas in all of personal lines coverage. Not because the concepts are complicated, but because almost nobody explains it clearly, and the marketing language on both sides makes it worse. Let me try to give you the real version.

## The Master Policy Is Not Your Policy

Your HOA carries a master policy that covers the building structure, common areas, liability in shared spaces, and sometimes more. The premiums come out of your HOA dues, which is probably why so many owners mentally file it under "I'm covered." But that policy is written to protect the association and the building, not you specifically.

What surprised me when I started pulling apart these policies as a claims adjuster was how dramatically the coverage scope varies depending on one term buried in the declarations page: whether the master policy is written on a "bare walls-in" or "all-in" (sometimes called "all-inclusive") basis.

Bare walls-in means the HOA policy covers the structure to the unfinished interior surfaces. The drywall itself might be covered, but your flooring, cabinets, countertops, light fixtures, appliances? That's all on you. All-in goes further, typically covering original fixtures and sometimes even improvements made by previous owners. The difference in what you'd owe out-of-pocket after a kitchen fire can be staggering.

Here's the problem: most condo owners have never actually read their association's master policy. A lot of them don't even know they can ask for a copy. You can. You should. Email your HOA board or management company and request a copy of the master policy declarations page. If they resist, your state's department of insurance can help clarify your rights.

There are three places where condo owners routinely get exposed, and I want to be specific about each one.

**The gap between the master policy deductible and your coverage.** HOA master policy deductibles have ballooned over the past decade. It's not unusual to see $10,000, $25,000, or even $50,000 deductibles on master policies now, driven by rising reinsurance costs after major weather events. If a pipe bursts and causes $30,000 in damage to the building, and the master policy has a $25,000 deductible, the association may come after unit owners to cover that gap. Your individual HO-6 condo policy can include "loss assessment coverage" to handle exactly this scenario. Most policies offer it as a relatively cheap add-on. Get it.

**Personal property and betterments.** Even a truly all-in master policy typically won't cover your furniture, clothing, electronics, or the upgraded hardwood floors you installed after you bought the unit. Those "betterments and improvements" are yours to insure. I've talked to owners who assumed their $8,000 custom tile work was covered under the master policy because it was "part of the building." It wasn't.

**The liability gap inside your unit.** If a guest slips on your wet kitchen floor, that's your liability exposure, not a common-area incident. The master policy covers the association; it doesn't step in to defend you personally for incidents inside your unit. Your HO-6 policy's liability coverage handles this. The standard limit is $100,000, which sounds like a lot until someone breaks an ankle and hires an attorney.

## Reading the Documents You Actually Need

Before you can even figure out what your HO-6 needs to cover, you need two documents: the master policy declarations and your condo association's CC&Rs (covenants, conditions, and restrictions). The CC&Rs will often spell out what the unit owner is responsible for maintaining and insuring. In some associations, you're responsible for the HVAC system inside your unit. In others, the association owns it. This affects both your insurance needs and your maintenance responsibilities.

One thing I'd recommend: once you get your hands on these documents, do a basic home inventory before you meet with your agent. There are solid apps for this now, like [Encircle](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=home+inventory+app&tag=yourtag-20) or even just video walking through every room and narrating what you own. This matters because underinsuring your personal property is extremely common, and without documentation, fighting a contents claim is an uphill battle. The [National Association of Insurance Commissioners](https://www.naic.org/) has a solid condo insurance buyer's guide that walks through the difference between HO-6 coverage types. It's dry reading, but it uses plain language and won't try to sell you anything.

## What Your HO-6 Policy Should Actually Cover

A well-built HO-6 policy for a condo should include:

- Dwelling coverage (for your interior finishes) calibrated to bare walls vs. all-in status of the master policy
- Personal property coverage at replacement cost, not actual cash value
- Loss of use coverage if you're temporarily displaced after a covered loss
- Personal liability of at least $300,000 (higher if you have meaningful assets)
- Loss assessment coverage of at least $50,000, given where master policy deductibles have gone
- Water backup coverage, which is usually excluded by default and worth adding

The research on whether extended replacement cost coverage matters for condos is mixed, since so much depends on how the master policy is structured. But for betterments and improvements specifically, replacement cost coverage almost always makes sense.

I'll also say this clearly: [IBHS home fortification research](https://ibhs.org/) is mostly aimed at single-family homes, but their guidance on water damage prevention applies directly to condos. A [water leak sensor](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=water+leak+sensor&tag=yourtag-20) near your water heater and under-sink plumbing costs around $20 to $40 and can stop a small drip from becoming a $40,000 claim that now involves your neighbors downstairs and your HOA's attorney.
