Your fence comes down in a windstorm. You file a claim. The adjuster tells you it's only covered up to $500 because your policy treats fencing as "other structures" and caps it at 10% of your dwelling coverage — except yours has a sublimit buried on page 31. I've seen this exact scenario play out dozens of times, and the homeowner is always blindsided. Not because they didn't read their policy, but because nobody told them fences sit in a strange middle ground of home insurance that most agents gloss over at signing.

So let's fix that.

## What "Other Structures" Actually Means for Your Fence

Standard homeowner policies (HO-3 is the most common form in the U.S.) cover fences under Coverage B, which is the "other structures" portion of your policy. The default limit for this coverage is 10% of your dwelling coverage amount. If your home is insured for $350,000, you've got $35,000 in other structures coverage on paper.

Sounds fine, right? Here's the problem. That $35,000 isn't just for your fence. It has to cover your detached garage, your shed, your pool enclosure, any retaining walls, your pergola, and anything else on your property that isn't the main house. If a tornado decides to remodel your backyard all at once, $35,000 can disappear fast.

What most people don't realize is that some policies layer sublimits on top of that. I've reviewed policies that cap fence coverage specifically at $500 or $1,000, regardless of what your Coverage B total says. These sublimits are legal, they're common in budget-tier policies, and they're almost never mentioned during the sales process. Pull out your declarations page and look for a line that says something like "fence" or "outdoor structures" with a separate dollar cap next to it. If you see one, that's the number that matters, not the big Coverage B figure.

This is where people get tripped up even when their coverage amount is fine.

Perils-based policies (and most HO-3s are "open perils" for the dwelling but "named perils" for other structures, which is a distinction that matters here) will typically cover your fence if it's damaged by:

- Wind and hail
- Fire
- Vandalism
- Falling objects (a tree limb, for instance)
- Vehicle impact
- Theft of fence materials (less common but sometimes included)

What they won't cover, almost universally: rot, gradual deterioration, insect damage, and general wear and tear. This sounds obvious until you have a fence that's 60% rotted and then a windstorm knocks it over. I've watched adjusters deny those claims by arguing the fence would have fallen anyway due to its pre-existing condition. They're not always wrong, and they're rarely wrong legally. A fence that was already failing doesn't get a free repair just because the wind finished the job.

Flooding is also excluded under a standard homeowner policy, full stop. If your fence washes away in a flash flood, you need a separate NFIP flood policy or private flood insurance for any chance of recovering that loss.

One more thing: neighbor situations. If your neighbor's tree falls on your fence, a lot of homeowners assume the neighbor is responsible. Generally, they're not, unless you can prove negligence (meaning you told them in writing the tree was dead or diseased and they ignored it). In most cases, you'd file with your own insurer and pay your deductible. Whether your insurer then pursues the neighbor's liability coverage is their problem, not yours. I know that feels backwards. It is, a little.

## The Actual Gaps You Need to Watch For

Let me be direct: the coverage gaps around fences are worse than most people expect, and the insurance industry has no incentive to advertise them.

**The deductible problem.** If your fence takes $1,800 in wind damage and you have a $2,000 deductible, you're paying out of pocket regardless of your coverage limits. Fences are one of those claims where the math often doesn't justify filing, because even if you're covered, the claim can affect your rates for years. A lot of adjusters I worked with privately advised homeowners to think hard before filing anything under about $3,000 to $4,000 in damage. That range shifts depending on your state and insurer, but the principle holds.

**Replacement cost vs. actual cash value.** If your policy settles other structures at actual cash value rather than replacement cost, you'll get a depreciation haircut on your fence payout. A 10-year-old wood privacy fence that costs $8,000 to replace might get valued at $3,500 after depreciation. Check your policy language for "ACV" versus "RCV" on Coverage B specifically. Some policies offer replacement cost on the dwelling but ACV on other structures. Read the fine print on both.

**Shared fences.** If you have a fence that sits exactly on the property line between you and your neighbor, things get complicated fast. Who owns it? Who's responsible for it? The answer varies by state and by whatever agreement (or lack of one) exists between you two. Most insurers will only cover the portion of the fence on your property, and sorting out ownership during a claim adds delays and arguments you don't want.

**HOA-owned fences.** If your subdivision has a fence that the HOA maintains, your homeowner policy almost certainly doesn't cover it. That's the HOA's problem, insured under their master policy. But if you've built a fence inside your lot that an HOA later damages during landscaping work, good luck getting anyone to pay quickly.

The [NAIC's consumer resources](https://www.naic.org/) have a useful breakdown of how other structures coverage works across different policy types if you want to compare what yours says against standard industry language.

## How to Actually Protect Yourself

Keep a home inventory that includes your fence: photos, measurements, the year it was installed, and what you paid (or an estimate of replacement cost today). I keep mine in a fire-rated document safe (affiliate link) and also backed up digitally. If you need to file a claim, this documentation is the difference between a smooth settlement and a three-week back-and-forth with an adjuster who's skeptical of your $9,000 replacement estimate.

If you have an expensive fence, a long perimeter, or a specialty fence (wrought iron, vinyl plank, a custom gate system), ask your agent specifically about your Coverage B sublimits and whether you can buy an endorsement to increase them. The cost is usually modest, maybe $15 to $40 a year, and it's worth it. Don't wait until after the storm.

The [IBHS fortification guides](https://ibhs.org/) also have recommendations for wind-resistant fence construction and anchoring that are worth reviewing if you're replacing or installing a fence in a high-wind area. Properly anchored fence posts can mean the difference between a fence that survives a 70 mph gust and one that turns into a projectile.
