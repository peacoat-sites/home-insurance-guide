Your tree just fell on your neighbor's fence. Or maybe their dead oak came down on your garage. Either way, you're standing in the yard staring at splintered wood wondering who pays for what, and you're probably getting two very different stories from two very different insurance agents.

After 14 years of adjusting claims, I've seen this scenario play out hundreds of times. And I'll tell you upfront: the rules here are counterintuitive, widely misunderstood, and almost never explained clearly at the point of sale.

Let me fix that.

## The Baseline Rule (That Surprises Almost Everyone)

Most people assume that if your property damages your neighbor's, you automatically owe them. That feels logical. It's also mostly not how insurance works.

Here's the standard position most home insurers take: each party's own insurance covers their own property. If your neighbor's tree falls on your fence, your homeowner's policy pays for your fence repair (minus your deductible), and your neighbor's policy isn't involved unless they were clearly negligent, meaning they knew the tree was dead or diseased and did nothing about it.

I tell people this is the hardest part to swallow, because it feels unfair. You did nothing wrong. A tree from next door destroyed your shed. And now you're eating a $1,000 deductible? Yes, potentially. That's how the system is designed.

The negligence piece matters enormously. If you sent your neighbor a certified letter six months ago warning them about that rotting oak, and they ignored it, you may have a legitimate liability claim against them. Keep documentation. Always. Without evidence of negligence, most adjusters, including me when I was still doing this work, will simply route the claim through the damaged party's own policy.

## What Your Liability Coverage Actually Does

Your homeowner's liability coverage (the "Coverage E" section of a standard HO-3 policy) protects you when you or your property causes bodily injury or property damage to someone else, and you're found legally responsible.

So if your retaining wall collapses and crushes your neighbor's car, and an engineer determines the wall was poorly maintained, your liability coverage steps in. Typical policies carry $100,000 in liability coverage. Many people would be well served by $300,000 or even a separate umbrella policy layered on top, especially if they have significant assets. The [Insurance Information Institute](https://www.iii.org/) has solid guidance on how to size liability limits to your actual net worth, and it's worth reading before your next renewal.

What liability coverage won't do: it won't cover intentional acts. It also won't cover damage to your own property. And it won't substitute for a neighborly conversation. I've watched small claims spiral into litigation over $3,000 of fence damage because two neighbors stopped talking and let lawyers do it instead.

One thing I see people get confused about: your liability coverage follows you personally, not just your home. If you're doing yardwork and accidentally throw a rock through their window with your mower, that's a liability situation too, not just a property damage claim against your own dwelling coverage.

## The Negligence Test: Where Every Disputed Claim Lives

This is where most neighbor damage disputes actually get decided. Was there negligence? That question will be answered by looking at:

What the property owner knew (or reasonably should have known) about the hazard. Whether they took reasonable steps to address it. How long the dangerous condition existed. Whether anyone warned them about it.

A healthy-looking tree that falls during a freak windstorm is generally considered an act of nature. Neither party is negligent. Both people file with their own insurers. End of story.

A tree your neighbor knew was hollow, that three arborists had flagged, that they'd been notified about twice in writing, is a different situation entirely. That's a winnable negligence claim. Whether it's worth pursuing depends on the dollar amount, your relationship with the neighbor, and honestly, your tolerance for conflict.

If you're in a disputed situation, your state's insurance department is a good first stop for understanding your rights before you hire an attorney. You can find your state's department through the [NAIC's state map](https://www.naic.org/state_web_map.htm). They handle consumer complaints and can sometimes clarify how your insurer is supposed to handle a specific type of claim.

## Proactive Steps That Actually Protect You

The best time to think about this is not while you're staring at a hole in your roof.

Document the condition of your property regularly. I recommend using a home inventory app like [Encircle](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=home+inventory+log+book) or even just a dated video walkthrough of your yard stored in the cloud. (The site may earn a commission.) This creates a baseline that becomes valuable if your own tree is ever accused of being a hazard.

If you have a large tree near the property line, get it inspected by a certified arborist every few years. The cost runs $100 to $300 depending on your area. If they identify disease or structural problems, get it in writing, then act on it. Removing a hazard tree typically costs $1,500 to $5,000, which hurts, but it's a fraction of what liability exposure looks like if that tree lands on someone's occupied structure.

If you're worried about a neighbor's tree, send a polite written notice. Not aggressive, just documented. Email works, but certified mail is better. If something does happen later, you'll want proof you raised it.
